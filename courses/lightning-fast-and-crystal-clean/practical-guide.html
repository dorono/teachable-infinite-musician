If you've made it all the way through all of the videos - let me first just say - CONGRATULATIONS! That is no small feat, since there is indeed a boatload of stuff we went through.

So now the question arises - "how do I actually fit all of this in my practice routine in a way that's deliberate and results-oriented?"

Well, I'm glad you asked - since here is the approach I've taken to mastering this content, and the pathway I'm laying out here definitely brought great results my way, and will do the same for you if you just stick with it and make mastery and fun the goal over any arbitrary deadline to master all of the lessons.

Phases

Rather than break down the steps in terms of time, I think it would be better to break it down in terms of content, so each phase here is to be completed on your own time table, depending on the time you have to practice as well as your experience level and skill.

Below you will see different phases with links to their associated video lessons, and in some cases, sheet music, along with some key points on how to practically integrate the phase into your practice routine.

Regardless of your level of skill, you should not attempt to touch upon every exercise in this course every day, or even every week. It will be much more practical for you to spend a portion of your practice time devoted to technique, focusing on one or more of the phases detailed below.

Depending on your skill level and time allotment for the “technique” portion of your practice routine, the number of phases practiced each day will vary drastically.

For continuity, I’ve done my best to divide all of the material below into the two primary ways that I think about the orientation of scales; horizontal and vertical.

While the non-chromatic exercises (ie: all phases excluding 13 and 14) described below are based on the major scale and its associated chords, the ultimate goal would be to repeat this sequence of phases with the exercises adjusted for other scales and associated chords including:

  • natural minor
  • melodic minor
  • harmonic minor
  • half diminished
  • diminished (aka octantonic)

Also keep in mind that this is just my suggested sequence of phases, but feel free modify the order of the phases if there are specific areas you want to tackle first, since the last thing I want to do is introduce rigity and zap the fun (and best possible results!) out of your technique practice.

Phase 1: Horizontal Aspects of Scales

VIDEO: Moving Horizontally Through Major
  • Strive to be able to play each scale in it's basic unaltered form in eighth notes at quarter note = 160, as a maximum tempo
  • Start the metronome at the fastest tempo you can play nearly 100% cleanly, no matter how slow that is

Phase 2: Vertical Aspect of Scales

VIDEO: Moving Vertically Through Major Scales
  • Diatonic Intervals
  • Begin with Diatonic Thirds. Once comfortable, move on to the next interval (4ths, 5ths, 6ths, etc)
  • One direction per week (can be adjusted to be more directions based upon comprehension level)
  • 3- 6 keys per week

Phase 3: Horizontal Aspect of Scales; Segmented

VIDEO: Scales in Different Directions
  • Begin with four-note segments. Once comfortable, being to practice five, six, seven note segments, etc.
  • One direction per week (can be adjusted to be more directions based upon comprehension level)
  • 3-6 keys per week

Phase 4: Vertical Aspect of Scales: Arpeggios

VIDEO: Practicing Arpeggios Using "Inners" and "Outers"
  • Although the name of this video indicates that we're working on "Inners" and "Outers", at this phase, we're just practicing just the arpeggios straight up and down, meaning playing a scale starting on the first note of that scale, skipping every other note, and then moving up to the next diatonic chord, starting on the root note of that chord and skipping every other note.
  • Diatonic 7th arpeggios to Major scales
  • 3-6 keys per week
  • Test knowledge of chord symbol and chord/scale relationship

Phase 5: Vertical Aspect of Scales: Creative approaches to 4th’s

VIDEO: Scales in Different Directions
  • Stacked 4ths, diatonic to a major key
  • 3 keys per week
  • One direction per week
  • Infinity 4th’s diatonic to a major key
  • 3 keys per week

Phase 6: Horizontal Aspect of Scales via Vertical Implications

VIDEO: Diatonic Intervals in Alternating Directions
  • Descending Major Scales by Ascending Diatonic 7th (Exercise Book : Page 10, Measure 222)
  • One Major Key per week
  • Eventually moving on to all modes diatonic to that one key, per week

Phase 7: Horizontal Aspect of Scales via Vertical Implications, Adjusted

VIDEO: Turning Your Technique Practice Into New Melodic Content for Your Solos
  • Descending Major Scales by Ascending Diatonic 7th, down a scale step (Exercise Book : Page 10, Measure 226)
  • One Major Key per week
  • Eventually moving on to all modes diatonic to that one key, per week

Phase 8: Horizontal Aspect of Scales via Vertical Implications, Integration

Integration of Phase 6 over a ii-V7-I progression (Horizontal Aspect of Scales via Vertical Implications)

  • 4 keys per week
  • Apply over a standard that you might be working on
